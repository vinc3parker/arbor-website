Hyrox has quietly become one of the most interesting things to happen to fitness in years.

It's a race, but it isn't really a running event. It's strength, but it isn't really a lifting competition. It's eight one-kilometre runs interspersed with eight functional stations — the ski erg, sled push and pull, burpee broad jumps, rowing, farmers carry, lunges, and wall balls. To do it well, you have to be a genuine hybrid athlete: someone who can run, carry, push, pull, and grind, all on the same day.

Which is exactly why building a **Hyrox training plan** is so hard.

Most training plans are built for one thing. A marathon plan assumes running is the priority and everything else is support. A strength program assumes the gym is the centre of your week. Hyrox refuses to sit neatly in either camp — and if you train it like a runner, your stations fall apart, and if you train it like a lifter, you blow up on the runs.

So here's how I think about structuring it.

---

## Start with the demand, not the schedule

The mistake I see most often is people building their week around *what they like doing* rather than what the race actually asks of them.

Hyrox demands three things at once: aerobic capacity to keep running, strength endurance to survive the stations, and the ability to switch between the two without falling apart. That last one — what people call "compromised running," running on legs that are already cooked — is the thing that catches everyone out.

A good plan trains all three, every week. Not in separate phases months apart, but together — because the race never lets you do one at a time.

## How to structure a Hyrox training week

For most people training around a job and a life, three to five sessions a week is realistic. A simple, sustainable shape looks like this:

**Two running-focused sessions.** One easy aerobic run to build your base, and one harder interval session — think 1km repeats at race pace with short rests, which mirrors exactly what the race feels like.

**Two strength sessions.** Keep these compound and functional: squats, deadlifts, lunges, presses, plus the carries and pulls that show up on the floor. You're building strength endurance, not a one-rep max.

**One "hybrid" session.** This is the one that matters most and the one people skip. Combine running and stations in the same workout — run, then sled, then run, then wall balls. Teach your body to run tired.

You don't need all five every week. But you do need the *blend* every week.

## Running and strength in the same plan

The fear with hybrid training is that the two sides cancel each other out — that running kills your strength and lifting wrecks your runs. It can happen, but usually it's a scheduling problem, not a physiology problem.

A few principles that keep it working:

- **Separate your hardest efforts.** Don't put your heaviest leg day the day before your hardest run. Give the big sessions room to breathe.
- **Easy days stay genuinely easy.** Most of your running should feel comfortable. The intensity is supposed to live in a small number of sessions, not all of them.
- **Respect recovery.** Hybrid training is more total load than either sport alone. Sleep and food aren't optional extras here — they're part of the plan.

None of this is complicated. But it's almost impossible to manage if your training is scattered across a running app, a separate gym tracker, and a note on your phone for the hybrid stuff.
